Many people want to start a call center business in 2026, but are unsure if it is still profitable. Rising costs, stricter regulations, and the growth of AI have created confusion about whether call centers are becoming obsolete or simply changing.

This confusion can be solved by looking at real market trends. Demand for call center services continues to grow in industries such as e-commerce, SaaS, healthcare, and finance. Meanwhile, cloud software and AI tools help reduce costs and improve agent productivity.

This article provides a practical, step-by-step guide to building a profitable call center business in 2026. You will learn which models work best, how much it costs to get started, which technology to use, and how to launch, optimize, and scale with confidence.

## Is a call center business still profitable in 2026?

![The global center market from 2025 to 2026](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/The-global-center-market-from-2025-to-2026-3.webp)

A call center business remains profitable in 2026 as customer support continues to be critical for companies competing on experience and trust. The global call center market is still expanding, valued at around [$440.8 billion in 2025](#) and projected to reach [$468.7 billion in 2026](#), showing steady demand for support services.

Despite rapid advances in AI and automation, human agents remain essential. AI is effective for handling routine tasks, but complex issues, emotional conversations, and revenue-driven interactions still require people. As a result, most companies use AI to support agents rather than replace them. This trend is reflected in the growth of AI tools, with the [AI call center](#) market expected to rise from [$3.98 billion in 2025](#) to $4.89 billion in 2026.

Demand is strongest in industries such as e-commerce, SaaS, healthcare, finance, and insurance, where reliable human support directly impacts satisfaction and retention. Outsourcing also continues to grow, as businesses seek cost efficiency and flexibility.

Profitability in 2026 depends on scale and efficiency. High call volumes, long-term contracts, labor arbitrage, and AI-assisted productivity help call centers remain competitive while controlling costs and improving margins.

## Types of call center businesses you can start

![Types of call center businesses you can start](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Types-of-call-center-businesses-you-can-start-2.webp)

When starting a call center business, choosing the right model is critical because it directly affects margins, risk level, and operational complexity. Broadly, call centers can be divided into inbound, outbound, hybrid, and niche-focused models.

-   **Inbound call centers** handle incoming calls from customers and are typically used for customer support, order processing, and technical support. This model offers relatively low margins but high stability, as demand is consistent and contracts are often long-term. Inbound services require strong service quality, trained agents, and solid systems, but sales pressure is minimal. This makes it a good entry point for beginners who prioritize predictable cash flow over rapid growth.
-   **Outbound call centers** focus on proactive calling, such as sales, telemarketing, and lead generation. These businesses usually enjoy higher margins, especially when paid per lead or per conversion. However, the risk is significantly higher. Performance fluctuates, agent turnover is common, and compliance issues (spam laws, call regulations) can quickly become a problem. This model suits founders with strong sales expertise and a higher risk tolerance.
-   **Hybrid call centers** combine inbound and outbound services. This approach works well when a business wants to maximize agent utilization; for example, handling inbound support during peak hours and outbound sales during slower periods. Hybrid models are ideal once operations are stable and management systems are mature.
-   **Niche call centers** specialize in specific industries, allowing operators to charge premium pricing. Examples include healthcare (appointment scheduling, patient follow-ups), real estate (lead qualification, showing coordination), e-commerce (order support, returns), and SaaS (onboarding, technical support). While niche call centers require deeper industry knowledge, they benefit from stronger client retention and less price competition.

## Call center business models (How you make money)

![Call center business models](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Call-center-business-models-1-2.webp)

Call centers generate revenue through several proven business models, each with different pricing structures, risk levels, and growth potential. Choosing the right model depends on your target clients, operational capacity, and long-term goals.

### BPO / Outsourced service model

The BPO (outsourced services) model is often the entry point for new call centers. In this setup, companies outsource customer support or sales operations to an external provider, paying only for the resources they use. Pricing is usually flexible, making this model attractive to small- and mid-sized clients.

The most common structure is per-agent pricing, where clients pay a fixed monthly fee per agent. This creates predictable income while allowing the call center to scale headcount as demand grows. Some clients prefer monthly retainers, which bundle services into a flat fee and ensure consistent cash flow regardless of minor volume fluctuations. For businesses with unpredictable demand, per-call or per-minute pricing is often used, shifting volume risk to the call center but offering clients maximum flexibility.

As client relationships deepen, many BPO providers transition into a more stable arrangement.

### Dedicated team model

The dedicated team model builds directly on the BPO structure by assigning a fixed group of agents to a single client. These engagements typically operate under long-term contracts, which allow both sides to invest in training, process optimization, and performance improvements.

Because staffing levels are fixed, this model delivers stable revenue and simplifies operational planning. Client commitment also leads to lower churn, as switching providers becomes costly and disruptive. While pricing may be less flexible than usage-based models, the predictability supports sustainable growth.

As margins become a priority, some call centers move toward technology-led approaches.

### SaaS-enabled call center model

SaaS-enabled call centers integrate cloud platforms to automate routing, reporting, and customer interactions. Revenue comes from a mix of software subscriptions and service fees. By reducing reliance on manual labor, this model supports a smaller team with higher margins, making it ideal for high-volume or digital-first clients.

### Franchise vs independent call center

Across all models, operators must choose between franchise and independent structures. Franchises offer proven systems and faster setup, but reduce control and profitability through fees. Independent call centers require more effort upfront but offer greater cost efficiency and strategic control over pricing, technology, and growth direction.

## Step-by-step: How to start a call center business

Starting a call center business requires strategic planning, compliance awareness, the right technology stack, and a strong operational foundation. This step-by-step guide walks you through how to build a scalable operation with effective [call center management](#) from day one.

### Step 1: Market research & niche selection

![Market research & niche selection step](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Step-1-2.webp)

Everything begins with understanding “Who is the call center?” and “why it should exist”. Before investing in people or tools, you need clarity on who you serve and what problem you solve.

Begin by narrowing down the industries that align with your capabilities and resources, such as:

-   e-commerce businesses needing order support and returns handling.
-   Healthcare providers require appointment scheduling and follow-ups.
-   SaaS companies are looking for technical or onboarding support.

Once you select an industry, look deeper to uncover unmet demand. This often appears in areas like:

-   Long wait times during peak hours.
-   Poor customer experience from outsourced support.
-   Lack of multilingual or after-hours coverage.

After identifying demand, analyze competitors already serving this space. Pay attention to their service scope, pricing, and customer complaints. These insights not only help differentiate your offering but also prepare you for tracking meaningful [call center metrics](#) later, such as response time and resolution rates.

### Step 2: Create a call center business plan

![Create a call center business plan step](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Step-2-2.webp)

With market clarity in place, the next step is building a business plan that connects strategy to execution. This plan acts as a practical guide rather than just a formal document.

Start by defining your value proposition. This should directly respond to the gaps you identified during market research. For example, your call center might focus on faster resolution, industry-trained agents, or flexible scaling during peak seasons.

Next, clearly define your target clients. Decide whether you are serving startups, small businesses, or mid-sized companies, since this choice affects everything from pricing to staffing models.

Pricing strategies should then align with both client expectations and operational costs. Common approaches include:

-   Per-agent monthly pricing for predictable workloads
-   Per-minute or per-call pricing for variable demand
-   Hybrid pricing that combines base fees with usage

Close the plan with financial projections. Estimate startup costs, monthly expenses, revenue targets, and the timeline to profitability. These projections ensure your business model is realistic and guide future investment decisions.

### Step 3: Legal setup & compliance

![Legal setup & compliance step](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Step-3-2.webp)

Once your business model is clear, formalizing the operation legally becomes the next logical step. Start with business registration and licensing according to local regulations. This establishes credibility and allows you to contract with clients. From there, create clear contracts and SLAs that define:

-   Service availability and response times.
-   Performance expectations and reporting.
-   Escalation procedures and penalties.

Compliance must be built into these agreements. Data protection and call recording laws, such as GDPR and TCPA, affect how you store customer data and monitor calls. Addressing compliance at this stage prevents operational disruptions later.

### Step 4: Choose the right call center technology

![Choose the right call center technology step](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Step-4-2.webp)

With legal and operational foundations in place, technology becomes the link between planning and daily performance.

Start with a reliable VoIP and telephony system that supports call routing, recording, monitoring, and remote agents. This infrastructure should be stable enough to handle growth without frequent disruptions.

Next, focus on CRM integration. When agents can access customer history during live calls, interactions become faster and more personalized, reducing repeat contacts and frustration. Efficient call flow depends on smart configuration, including:

-   Call routing to direct callers to the right agent.
-   IVR systems to manage simple requests automatically.

As your operation matures, AI and automation tools can support forecasting, sentiment analysis, and self-service options. Using a call center software buying guide at this stage helps compare vendors, avoid overpaying for unused features, and choose systems that scale with your business.

### Step 5: Hire & train agents

![Hire & train agents step](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Step-5-1.webp)

Once systems are ready, attention shifts to people. Agents are the voice of your brand, and their performance directly affects client retention.

Hire candidates with strong [call center skills](#) such as communication, empathy, adaptability, and problem-solving. While tools and scripts can be taught, the ability to handle customers calmly and professionally is essential from day one.

Training should be structured and continuous. A strong training framework typically includes system usage, compliance requirements, call handling techniques, and soft skills development. Quality assurance processes like call monitoring, feedback sessions, and coaching help maintain consistent service standards as call volumes increase.

### Step 6: Launch, test, and optimize

![Launch, test, and optimize step](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Step-6-1.webp)

The final step brings everything together through a controlled launch. Rather than going all in immediately, start with pilot campaigns to test workflows, staffing levels, and technology performance.

Use early results to refine call scripts and workflows. Agents should follow structured processes while still sounding natural and conversational. Small adjustments at this stage can significantly improve customer experience.

Finally, track performance closely using essential call center metrics such as average handle time, first-call resolution, customer satisfaction, and agent utilization. These insights allow you to identify inefficiencies early and continuously improve operations, setting the stage for confident scaling and long-term success.

## How much does it cost to start a call center business?

![How much does it cost to start a call center business](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/cost-to-start-a-call-center-business-1.webp)

The cost of starting a call center business depends mainly on your team size and operating model, but most expenses follow the same lifecycle: upfront setup costs followed by recurring monthly expenses. At a general level, launching a call center typically requires $10,000–$30,000 for a small setup and $40,000–$80,000 for a medium-sized operation.

Before operations begin, you’ll need to cover several startup costs that make the business functional. These typically include:

-   Business registration and legal setup
-   Hardware and equipment for agents
-   Call center software and system configuration
-   Initial hiring and agent training

Taken together, startup costs usually range from $10,000–$25,000 for a 5-agent call center, while a 20-agent operation may require $40,000–$70,000 upfront, depending on location and technology choices.

Once the call center is live, expenses shift toward monthly operating costs, many of which scale directly with the number of agents you employ. These ongoing costs include salaries and benefits, software subscriptions, telecom and internet services, and office or remote infrastructure.

On average, monthly operating costs range from $12,000–$20,000 for a 5-agent team. For a 20-agent call center, monthly expenses typically fall between $45,000–$80,000. While larger teams require more capital, they benefit from better cost efficiency per agent as fixed expenses are spread across a larger workforce.

## Essential technology stack for a call center business

A successful call center depends on a modern, integrated technology stack that supports scalability, efficiency, and consistent service quality. Key components include:

-   **Cloud-based call center software:** This is the backbone of operations, enabling inbound and outbound calling, IVR, call routing, omnichannel communication, and remote work. Cloud platforms reduce infrastructure costs, scale easily, and provide real-time analytics and reporting.
-   **CRM systems:** CRM software centralizes customer profiles, interaction history, and case data. When integrated with call center platforms, it gives agents instant caller context, improving personalization, reducing average handle time, and increasing first-call resolution rates.
-   **Workforce management (WFM) tools:** WFM tools help forecast call volumes, create optimized schedules, track agent adherence, and manage productivity. They ensure proper staffing levels, balancing customer demand with operational costs.
-   **Call monitoring & QA tools:** These tools record and evaluate calls to maintain compliance and service standards. Supervisors can score interactions, identify training needs, and continuously improve agent performance.
-   **AI-powered tools**, including:
    -   Call summaries for automated documentation.
    -   Sentiment analysis to detect customer emotions in real time.
    -   Agent assist features that suggest responses, surface knowledge articles, and guide next-best actions.

Together, these technologies create a scalable, data-driven call center operation.

## FAQ

### How many agents do I need to start?

You can start a call center with as few as 2–5 agents, especially if you focus on a niche or pilot campaign. Small teams reduce risk, make training easier, and allow you to validate demand before scaling. Many successful call centers grow gradually once workflows and quality standards are proven.

### Can I run a remote call center?

Yes. Many call centers in 2026 operate fully or partially remote. Cloud-based call center software, VoIP, and performance monitoring tools enable easy management of remote agents while maintaining quality, security, and compliance. Remote setups also lower overhead costs and expand your hiring pool.

### What is the best call center software for startups?

The best software for startups is cloud-based, easy to scale, and priced on a per-user basis. Look for platforms that offer VoIP, CRM integration, call routing, reporting, and basic AI features. Popular choices for startups prioritize fast setup, transparent pricing, and flexibility as your team grows.
