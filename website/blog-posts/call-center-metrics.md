We all know that data is power, but in the world of customer support, it can also be a massive headache. Call center metrics are the measurements we use to track performance and customer experience, but using them correctly is harder than it looks.

It’s easy to get it wrong by tracking too many irrelevant KPIs, sacrificing your customers’ happiness just to lower handle times, or gathering great data that just sits in a report gathering dust.

If you’re tired of guessing which numbers actually move the needle, you’re in the right place. We have compiled a comprehensive list of 26 metrics that matter most, helping you spot the difference between “vanity metrics” and the ones that truly drive success. Let’s get started!

## Why call center metrics matter

Running a support team without data is like driving with a painted-over windshield: you’re moving, but you can’t see the wall you’re about to hit. Metrics are the only tools that reveal the true state of your operations, turning guesswork into an actionable strategy.

Tracking these numbers is critical for three reasons:

-   **Visibility into performance:** You can’t manage what you don’t measure. Metrics clearly show which agents need coaching and which processes are broken, providing the factual clarity needed for effective [call center management](https://meetchatty.com/blog/call-center-management).
-   **Early warning signals:** Metrics act like a smoke alarm, flagging issues like rising queue times or repeat calls before they damage your reputation. This allows you to fix problems early, preventing the frustration that leads to lost customers.
-   **Balancing operations, CX, and cost:** Data aligns competing demands. By tracking the right numbers, you can ensure your call center stays profitable and efficient without ever sacrificing customer experience quality.

## How to organize call center metrics (the framework most teams miss)

![call center metrics category](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/call-center-metrics-category.webp)

Here are 5 practical categories most teams should use:

1.  **Speed and access metrics:** How easy it is for customers to reach you, such as hold time and abandonment rate.
2.  **Efficiency and cost metrics:** How well you use resources, such as calls per hour and cost per contact.
3.  **Quality and resolution metrics:** Whether issues are truly solved, such as first contact resolution and repeat contacts.
4.  **Customer experience metrics:** How customers feel after the interaction, such as CSAT or NPS.
5.  **Agent performance metrics:** How reliably agents show up and follow the plan, such as schedule adherence and QA scores.

## Speed and access call center metrics

### Average speed of answer (ASA)

![Average speed of answer formula showing ASA calculated as total waiting time for answered calls divided by the total number of answered calls](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/average-speed-of-answer-metric.webp)

This metric measures the average time a customer waits in the queue before an agent actually picks up the phone. It is the most direct indicator of your team’s accessibility.

A healthy ASA typically falls between 20-30 seconds for phone support. If your ASA is consistently high, it usually signals that you are understaffed or that your agents are getting bogged down in complex issues that should have been routed elsewhere. If you want to sanity-check targets like ASA, Service Level, or AHT by industry and channel, use [customer support benchmarking](https://meetchatty.com/blog/customer-support-benchmarking) to compare your baseline before you push any optimization.

### Service level

Service level sets your standard for how fast calls get answered. It is usually the percentage of calls answered within a target time, like the common 80/20 rule (80% answered in 20 seconds). But it can hide pain. You can hit 80/20 while the slowest 20% wait 10 minutes. If service level looks fine but CSAT drops, audit the worst-wait group.

### Average queue time

While ASA tracks the time for _answered calls_, Average Queue Time often includes the time spent by callers who eventually hang up. This makes it a more honest reflection of the total “waiting pain” your customers experience. If ASA looks low but queue time is high, you likely have a dropout problem. Many callers wait too long and abandon, so only the “lucky” fast answers show up in ASA.

### Call abandonment rate

![Call abandonment rate formula illustrating how to calculate the percentage of inbound calls abandoned before being answered by an agent](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/call-abandonment-rate-metric.webp)

This is the percentage of callers who hang up before reaching an agent. A high abandonment rate usually signals a mismatch between call demand and your available staff, or a confusing IVR (Interactive Voice Response) menu that frustrates people before they even enter the queue.

Priority actions to fix high abandonment:

-   **Check your IVR:** Is your menu too long or complicated? Simplify options to get callers into the queue faster.
-   **Offer callbacks:** Give customers the option to have an agent call them back instead of waiting on hold.
-   **Adjust staffing:** Use smart scheduling to ensure more agents are available during peak hours.

### Blocked call rate

![Call center blocked call percentage calculation showing how to measure calls that do not reach an agent relative to total incoming calls](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/blocked-call-percentage-metric-calculation.webp)

A blocked call happens when a customer cannot enter the queue because all lines or the system capacity are full. They hear a busy tone or “try again later.” This matters because blocked callers do not appear in your wait time or ASA reports, so demand looks lower than it is. If you ignore it, you will under-plan staffing and miss hidden revenue, since each blocked call can be a lost sale or a churn risk.

### Callback acceptance rate

The callback acceptance rate shows how many callers choose a callback when you offer it instead of waiting on hold. It tells you whether your callback option is truly reducing friction. A high rate usually supports CSAT because customers trust the promise, hang up, and use their time elsewhere. When you call back on time, the conversation starts calmer than a call that begins after a long hold.

## Efficiency and cost call center metrics

### Average handle time (AHT)

![Average handle time calculation diagram showing how AHT is measured using talk time, hold time, and after-call work divided by the number of customer interactions](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/average-handle-time-metric-explanation.webp)

AHT measures the total duration of a customer interaction, including talk time, hold time, and any wrap-up work. While many managers try to push this number down to save money, a low AHT is not always a victory.

If agents rush customers to get off the phone quickly, they often fail to solve the real problem, leading to repeat calls. AHT should always be viewed alongside First Contact Resolution (FCR). If AHT goes up but FCR also improves, that is usually a smart trade-off: spending two extra minutes now to save a ten-minute callback later.

### After-call work (ACW)

ACW tracks the time an agent spends finishing tasks after the customer hangs up, such as logging notes or updating the CRM.

High ACW often signals a problem with your tools, not your people. If agents are spending 5 minutes navigating clunky software for every 3-minute call, you have a process failure. The goal is to optimize workflows (using automation or better integration), so agents can focus on talking, not typing, without feeling pressured to cut corners on data quality.

### Cost per contact

![Cost per contact formula showing total cost of handling customer inquiries divided by total number of customer inquiries](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/cost-per-contact-metric-formula.webp)

This metric calculates the total expense of running your support center divided by the number of contacts handled. It is a favorite of finance teams but needs context to be useful, which is why teams should define a small set of primary KPIs first, then use cost per contact as a secondary lens.

A shortlist of [customer support KPIs you need to track](https://meetchatty.com/blog/customer-support-kpis-need-track) helps you set that foundation, so cost is not the only thing you optimize.

_When to use it?_

-   Comparing channels, such as phone vs chat, after normalizing for similar issue types.
-   Budgeting and forecasting staffing cost impacts across seasons.
-   Evaluating self-service or automation investments, before and after rollout.
-   Benchmarking an outsourced vendor against your internal operation.

This metric can be misleading because it treats every interaction the same. A 2-minute password reset is cheap, while a 30-minute fraud investigation is expensive but saves the company millions. Focusing solely on driving this cost down can lead to understaffing complex queues, ultimately hurting the business.

### Occupancy rate

![Call center occupancy rate formula showing total handling time divided by total handling time plus available time](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/call-center-occupancy-rate-formula.webp)

Occupancy measures the percentage of time agents spend actively handling calls versus waiting for work. It answers the question: “How busy is my team?”

While you want agents to be productive, pushing occupancy too high is dangerous. An occupancy rate consistently above 85-90% means agents have zero breathing room between calls. This is a clear burnout signal. If agents are back-to-back all day, they become exhausted, error-prone, and likely to quit, which costs far more than hiring an extra person to lower the load.

### Utilization rate

![Call center utilization rate formula showing total logged-in time divided by total shift time to measure agent workload](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/call-center-utilization-rate-formula.webp)

Utilization and occupancy sound similar, but they answer different questions. **Occupancy** is how much of logged-in time agents spend handling work. **Utilization** looks at a wider slice of paid time, which can include training, coaching, and meetings.

Common mistakes when you mix the two:

-   Using an occupancy formula but calling it utilization
-   Benchmarking against other teams without matching definitions
-   Cutting training as “unproductive” time
-   Forgetting shrinkage, then blaming agents for “low” utilization

A useful way to use utilization is to define what counts as productive paid time in your operation, then keep the definition stable so you can trend it over time.

### Schedule adherence

![Call center schedule adherence formula showing minutes in adherence divided by total scheduled minutes, multiplied by 100](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/schedule-adherence-performance-metric.webp)

Schedule adherence measures whether agents follow their assigned schedule, meaning they are logged in and available when planned. Low adherence is often blamed on agents, but it can be a management issue.

If volumes spike and agents cannot get off calls in time for breaks, the real problem is forecasting or staffing, not discipline. Strong adherence comes from realistic schedules that include shrinkage, break buffers, and coverage for unexpected surges.

## Quality and resolution call center metrics

### First call resolution (FCR)

![First Call Resolution calculation showing resolved cases divided by total cases, with an example demonstrating a 75 percent FCR](https://chatty.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/first-call-resolution-calculation.webp)

FCR measures the percentage of calls where the customer’s issue is completely resolved without them needing to call back, be transferred, or wait for a follow-up.

FCR is famously hard to measure because “resolved” is subjective. An agent might mark a ticket as closed, but the customer might still be confused and call back two days later. Despite this difficulty, FCR is widely considered the “king of metrics” because it is the single strongest predictor of customer satisfaction. When you solve a problem in one go, costs drop and loyalty skyrockets.

### Repeat contact rate

This metric is the mirror image of FCR. It tracks the percentage of customers who contact you more than once regarding the same issue within a set timeframe (e.g., 7 days).

Repeat contact rate is essential for catching what FCR misses. It acts as a safety net for your data: if your agents claim a 90% FCR but your Repeat Contact Rate is 30%, you know your team is closing tickets prematurely. It is the best way to spot “half-solved” problems where an agent provided a quick fix that didn’t last.

### Transfer rate

This measures the percentage of calls an agent transfers to another department or support tier.

Not all transfers are bad. A “warm transfer” to a specialist who can actually help is far better than an agent guessing the answer. However, a high transfer rate usually signals broken routing logic. It often means your IVR is sending customers to the wrong people.

When to adjust routing:

-   Customers frequently ask, _“Is this the right department?”_
-   Tier 1 agents are transferring basic queries because they lack the necessary permissions (e.g., refunds).
-   Transfer rates spike in specific topics (e.g., billing questions landing in tech support).

### Reopen rate

Common in ticket-based support (like email or helpdesk), this measures how often a ticket marked “solved” is reopened by the customer.

A high reopen rate is a clear sign of poor post-handling quality. It often means agents are prioritizing speed over thoroughness, sending a generic “article link” response to close the ticket quickly, only for the customer to reply, _“This didn’t help”_.

### Quality assurance (QA) score

A QA score grades an agent’s interaction using a checklist of factors such as greeting, tone, compliance, and solution accuracy. It becomes risky when it sits in a bubble. An agent can follow the script perfectly and still frustrate the customer.

To make QA useful, tie it to CX outcomes and score what drives resolution, clarity, and trust. If you need a practical rubric template, start with a [customer service scorecard](https://meetchatty.com/blog/customer-service-scorecard). Calibration is also mandatory. If two supervisors score the same call 90% vs 60%, the data is not reliable. Regular calibration keeps scoring consistently.

## Customer experience call center metrics

### Customer satisfaction (CSAT)

CSAT measures how satisfied a customer feels right after an interaction. Its biggest strength is speed. You get fast feedback on specific calls, so you can spot issues early. But CSAT is emotional and subjective. A customer may score low because they hate a policy, not because the agent did a poor job.

To make CSAT actionable, segment it by call reason, like returns vs technical support, to see where dissatisfaction really comes from.

### Customer effort score (CES)

CES measures how easy it was for a customer to get their issue solved. In call centers, it often predicts loyalty better than CSAT because effort drives churn. If a caller gets transferred, repeats details, or struggles to reach the right team, they will leave even if the agent is polite. Lowering effort usually reduces repeat contacts, too, because customers get the right help the first time.

### Net promoter score (NPS)

Unlike CSAT or CES, NPS is a long-term loyalty metric, not a transactional one. It reflects the customer’s overall relationship with your brand, not just their last phone call. Therefore, do not use NPS to measure a single agent’s performance on a Tuesday morning. Use it to guide broader strategic decisions about your brand’s reputation.

### Sentiment score

Thanks to AI, you no longer have to wait for survey results to know how customers feel. Sentiment analysis tools listen to voice and tone in real-time to detect frustration. This offers a significant advantage over traditional surveys, which typically only capture the “vocal minority”: the very happy or the very angry.

### Complaint rate

Do not just count complaints; categorize them. If 50% of complaints are about “rude agents,” but your QA scores are high, your QA criteria are wrong. If complaints are about “long wait times,” your forecasting is off. Treat every formal complaint as a free consulting report on where your operation is broken.

## Agent performance and stability metrics

### Agent attrition rate

Attrition measures the percentage of agents leaving your company over a specific period. It is often the strongest leading indicator of a deteriorating Customer Experience (CX).

High attrition harms CX because you are constantly replacing experts with novices. It also creates a vicious cycle: when agents leave, the remaining team gets overworked (higher occupancy), leading to more burnout and more quitting. Always link attrition data to scheduling stress and QA scores to see if you are burning people out or failing to support them.

### Absenteeism rate

This tracks the percentage of scheduled shifts that agents miss. Unplanned absences destroy your Service Level. If you forecasted for 50 agents and 5 call in sick, the remaining 45 face higher volumes, wait times skyrocket, and the day starts in crisis mode. If you see high absenteeism, look at your culture and scheduling practices before blaming external factors.

### Agent productivity per hour

This measures how much “work” (calls, tickets, chats) an agent handles per hour. To measure correctly:

-   **Weight by complexity:** Do not just count raw numbers. An agent handling 10 complex tech-support calls is far more productive than one rushing through 20 simple password resets.
-   **Avoid bad incentives:** Focusing solely on raw volume creates “cherry-picking” behavior, where agents intentionally avoid hard tickets just to keep their stats high.

### Coaching effectiveness score (derived)

A coaching effectiveness score shows whether coaching actually changed performance, not just whether coaching happened.

How to build it:

-   Pick one target KPI tied to a call reason, like fewer billing transfers or higher FCR for delivery delays.
-   Set a baseline using each agent’s last 2 to 4 weeks.
-   Measure the next 2 to 4 weeks, then recheck at 6 to 8 weeks to confirm it sticks.
-   Score the lift: post KPI minus pre KPI, with a guardrail like “no CSAT drop.”
-   If possible, compare against a similar uncoached group to control for seasonality.

## How call center metrics work together

Isolated metrics often tell a misleading story. To truly understand the health of your call center, you must analyze how different numbers interact as follows:

-   **AHT ↓ + FCR ↓ → Rushed calls:** When both metrics drop, agents are likely sacrificing quality for speed. They rush customers off the phone to meet time targets, causing repeat calls and unresolved issues.
-   **Service level ↑ + occupancy ↑ → Burnout risk:** High speed combined with high busyness means agents have zero recovery time between calls. This relentless pace is a primary driver of burnout and future attrition.
-   **QA ↑ + CSAT ↓ → QA rubric misaligned:** If internal scores are high but customers are unhappy, you are measuring the wrong things. Your scorecard likely prioritizes compliance (like script reading) over actual problem-solving.

## How to improve call center metrics

Below are 4 moves to improve call center metrics. Start with access, then fix resolution, then speed up execution, and finally use AI to remove busywork.

-   **Forecast and schedule for peaks:** Pull 14 days of calls by 30-minute blocks, add shrinkage, move breaks away from the top 2 peak blocks, and add one short surge buffer shift to lift service level and cut average queue time.
-   **Route calls to the right team first:** Define top 10 call reasons, map each to one primary queue and one allowed transfer path, then simplify IVR to one intent question to raise FCR and lower transfers, and repeat contacts.
-   **Standardize the fix with a 2-minute knowledge play:** For the top issues, write short KB pages with “when to use” and steps, attach the right macro, and place the KB link inside the ticket form to reduce AHT and ACW without hurting CSAT.
-   **Use AI for intake and after-call notes:** Let an AI chatbot collect order ID and reason before handoff and auto-generate a call summary so agents spend less time typing, which lowers ACW and improves resolution consistency.

Ultimately, call center metrics are only as useful as the actions we take to improve them. Instead of chasing perfect scores across the board, we should use these numbers to spot burnout, fix broken processes, and make life easier for our agents.

## FAQs

### How many call center metrics should you track?

Aim for 5 to 7 core KPIs tied to today’s goals. Add extra metrics only when someone owns them, and you know what action to take. Too many numbers create noise and slow decisions.

### What’s the difference between metrics and KPIs?

A metric is any measurable number, like ASA or transfer rate. A KPI is a metric tied to a goal and a target, like service level 80/20 or FCR above your threshold.

### How do call center metrics improve customer experience?

They surface friction early, like rising queue time, more transfers, or repeat contacts. That helps you fix staffing, routing, and training fast. When effort drops, and issues are resolved on the first try, CSAT and loyalty usually rise.
