# Market & Competitor Research Methodology

## 1. Define the Research Scope

Start by clarifying what you're trying to learn. There are three distinct research types:

**Market research** answers "what do customers need and how big is the opportunity?" Focus on understanding pain points, willingness to pay, and market sizing.

**Competitive research** answers "who else solves this problem and how?" Focus on positioning, features, pricing, and go-to-market strategies.

**Competitive intelligence** answers "what are competitors doing now and planning next?" Focus on tracking changes, new releases, hiring patterns, and strategic moves.

---

## 2. Primary Research Methods

**Customer interviews** remain the highest-signal source. Structure interviews around jobs-to-be-done rather than feature requests. Ask "what were you trying to accomplish when you installed [competitor]?" rather than "what features do you want?"

**Win/loss analysis** is underutilized. When merchants churn or choose a competitor during evaluation, capture why. Even 5-10 of these conversations reveal patterns.

**Sales call listening** surfaces objections and comparison points in real-time. Track "why did they mention [competitor]?" in CRM notes to build a living competitive dataset.

---

## 3. Secondary Research Framework

Build a systematic collection process across these sources:

### Product Intelligence
- Monitor competitor changelogs, app store updates, and product announcements
- Track app listing changes weekly—description updates often telegraph positioning shifts
- Use tools like Feedly or a Notion database for collection

### Positioning Intelligence
- Analyze homepage messaging, comparison pages, case studies, and ad copy
- Note which customer segments they highlight
- Identify which pain points they lead with

### Financial/Scale Intelligence
- Employee count on LinkedIn (track growth over time)
- Job postings (reveal strategic priorities)
- Funding announcements
- Review volume trends on app stores

### Content/SEO Intelligence
- What keywords are they targeting?
- What content themes dominate?
- This reveals their demand-gen strategy

## 4. Competitive Analysis Frameworks

### Feature Matrix
Table stakes but easy to do poorly. Don't just list features—weight them by customer importance. A feature that matters to 5% of prospects shouldn't carry equal weight.

### Positioning Map
Place competitors on 2x2 axes relevant to buying decisions. Examples:
- Automation depth vs. ease of setup
- SMB focus vs. enterprise focus

### Jobs-to-be-Done Analysis
Map which jobs each competitor optimizes for. Example:
- Gorgias → "respond to tickets efficiently at scale"
- Tidio → "convert visitors through chat"

These aren't the same job, even though products overlap.

### Value Curve Analysis
Plot competitors against key buying criteria to find gaps and differentiation opportunities (from Blue Ocean Strategy).

---

## 5. Synthesis and Action

Raw research is useless without synthesis. Create three outputs:

| Output | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Competitive Battlecards** | One page per major competitor for sales/support—positioning, strengths, weaknesses, how to win |
| **Market Opportunity Map** | Underserved segments or unmet needs |
| **Strategic Watch List** | 3-5 competitors to track monthly with specific signals |

---

## 6. Research Cadence

| Frequency | Activity |
|-----------|----------|
| **Monthly** | Competitor tracking (app updates, pricing changes, new features) |
| **Quarterly** | Deep-dives on 1-2 competitors |
| **Annually** | Market sizing refresh |
